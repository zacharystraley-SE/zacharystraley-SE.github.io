Sales Engineering / Video Production

How I produced the video that helped win a $1.5M FIFA contract.

This is a walkthrough of how I made a video submission for FIFA's open RFP for queuing services at the 2026 World Cup. The submission helped my team win a $1.5M contract. The page exists because the video itself belongs to my former employer, but the process behind it is mine.

I
IBrief

Turning the requirements into a plan.

FIFA's RFP was about eight pages of technical requirements for a queuing vendor. The default approach is to answer them in the order they're listed. I went a different way. I read through every requirement and assigned each one to a specific moment in the customer journey, from booking an appointment all the way through to the post-visit feedback survey. By the time I was done, every requirement had a home inside a story the video would already need to tell.

The Qmatic customer journey
II
IIStrategy

Designing the video to actually hold attention.

Most vendor demo videos answer technical requirements one at a time. Feature, check, next feature. It works, but it's boring to watch, and the people reviewing these submissions are watching a lot of them. I decided to build an actual narrative around the requirements instead. The video would still cover everything FIFA asked about, but it would do it in the natural flow of a story rather than as a checklist. My experience producing YouTube content has taught me a lot about what keeps a viewer watching, and this was a chance to apply that directly to the work.

III
IIIScript

Writing the script in three passes.

With the requirement mapping done, I start writing. The first pass is a loose outline. Just enough structure to see the shape of the story and decide what order the beats should go in. Once that feels right, I go back through and add bullet points under each main section: what I'll cover there, how long I think I'll spend on it, what the transition to the next section looks like. Once the outline is structured, I bring it to Claude and have it organize my thoughts into a clean, performable script. From there I review, make adjustments, and the script is ready for voiceover.

IV
IVProduction

How I assemble the video.

With the script finalized, I start with voiceover. I record into DaVinci Resolve at a comfortable pace and leave deliberate dead space between sentences. That way, if I flub a line, I can re-record just that sentence without having to re-align the waveform with everything that came before.

Once the VO is done, I put my headphones on, play it back, and start screen recording in time with my own voice. I'm walking through software I've used a thousand times, but now I'm doing it to the rhythm of the script. I'm careful to keep mouse movements smooth and deliberate. Jerky cursor work disorients viewers and breaks the spell of a polished demo.

I capture footage with OBS, ShareX, or QuickTime depending on what machine I'm on. All of them dump into a folder that auto-syncs with my DaVinci project and refreshes every three minutes. Start and stop are hotkeyed. The faster I can capture and re-capture, the more I can iterate without losing focus.

Then I assemble. I lay the screen recordings down on the timeline so that what's happening visually matches what I'm saying at every beat. After a rough assembly, I watch the video multiple times. Not to look for technical mistakes. I'm watching for moments where I would lose interest as a viewer.

Anything that doesn't serve the story gets cut, even when it's something I worked hard on. That's probably the hardest part of being an above-average editor. Knowing when to cut a clip you're proud of because it doesn't actually move the story forward. At the end of the day, the only thing that matters is whether every second supports the core mission of the video. If a moment doesn't, it's dead space, and it gets cut.

V
VPost

How I finish the video.

With a clean rough cut in place, post-production is where the video starts feeling like real media instead of a screen recording with narration.

If I shot any footage on my own camera, I color grade it during this stage so it matches the rest of the video. I add subtle sound effects throughout. Not to show off, but because every movie I've ever watched does the same thing. Engaging more than one of the viewer's senses makes a piece of media more memorable, and a more memorable pitch has a better chance of closing a sale.

I use custom macros and plugins for motion graphics, zoom animations, and effects that draw the viewer's attention to specific parts of the screen. Sometimes that means highlighting an element. Sometimes it means blurring or masking everything around it.

The last step happens in DaVinci's Fairlight tab, which is where the audio gets its final treatment. I scan the high frequencies of my voice and cut anything that sounds harsh or boxy. I add a light voice isolation pass to clean up the room. And I run a DeEsser to handle any plosives that survived the recording.

VI
VIDelivery

How I deliver the final video.

With the final cut done, I export the video in H.265 ProRes. That codec preserves the quality of the motion graphics without producing a file size that's impractical to share. The export goes to a Dropbox folder, and I set the sharing permissions to match whatever the RFP specified. Once the upload finishes, I send the link to the vendor.
